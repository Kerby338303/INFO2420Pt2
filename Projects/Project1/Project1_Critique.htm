Web Site Critiques

Evaluation Questions

The following questions are used to evaluate a single web site.

  1. What was your first impression of this site?
  2. How does this site organize the flow of information?
  3. Does the site structure make sense or does it confuse you? Why?
  4. Does the site graphics/layout/multimedia enhance the site or detract from it? Why?
  5. How long did it take for the home page to load? Was the load time reasonable?
  6. Was the text color and size readable? If a background color or image was used, did it enhance or detract from the site?
  7. Did the site have a navigation bar or other navigation tool? Was the navigation well done?
  8. Did the site have a color scheme or look and feel that was repeated on all the pages?
  9. How does the site respond to smaller window sizes (responsive design)?
  10. What does this site offer that was unusual or very well done?

Trader Joe’s — http://www.traderjoes.com/

1. My first impression of the Trader Joe’s home page is that it feels friendly and inviting. The design uses large photos of food and a handwritten-style typeface that matches the fun, quirky brand image. It feels like a grocery store that wants to be approachable rather than corporate.

2. The site organizes information with a traditional structure: a main navigation bar across the top with links such as Products, Recipes, and Locations. Below that, the page is divided into sections with headings and images that highlight seasonal items or featured stories. Content generally flows from top navigation, to large hero area, to smaller feature blocks lower on the page.

3. Overall the structure makes sense. It is easy to understand where to go if I want to find a store or browse products. The only mild confusion comes from some promotional panels that look similar to navigation, so I have to pause for a moment to tell what is a link to a main section and what is just a featured article.

4. The graphics and layout mostly enhance the site. High-quality food photography makes the products look appealing, and the illustrated textures in the background add personality. There are a lot of visual elements, though, so on a smaller screen the page can feel a little busy. Still, the images support the brand and make the site more interesting than a plain grocery listing.

5. On a typical connection the home page loads in just a few seconds. There are several large images, but they appear to be optimized so they do not slow things down too much. For a modern, image-heavy site the load time feels reasonable and would not annoy most users.

6. The text is readable. Most of the body text uses a clean sans-serif font on a white or very light background, which gives good contrast. Headings sometimes use a script or decorative typeface, but those are kept large enough that they are still legible. The occasional textured or colored background behind text is subtle, so it does not interfere with readability.

7. The site includes a clear navigation bar at the top of every page. The navigation items are grouped logically and behave consistently when hovered or tapped. There is also a search box that helps users jump directly to what they want. Overall, the navigation is straightforward and easy to use.

8. The color scheme and overall look and feel are consistent across the site. Trader Joe’s uses warm reds, oranges, and tan colors along with the same fonts and illustration style throughout its pages. This repetition helps users know they are still on the same site, even when they move to a different section.

9. When the browser window is made smaller, the layout adjusts in a reasonably responsive way. The top navigation collapses into a menu icon, and content stacks vertically. Some of the promotional images become large vertical blocks on a phone, so there is more scrolling, but the site still works and remains usable on a smaller screen.

10. One thing the site does especially well is using storytelling around products. Instead of just listing groceries, the site features articles and recipe ideas that show how to use the items. This combination of product information, store locations, and lifestyle content makes the site feel more like a magazine than a simple store catalog, which is unusual for a grocery chain.
